Theme of the Sunday: I Want Love, Not Sacrifices.     In the first reading and in the gospel we find the phrase, “I want deeds of love and mercy, not sacrifice!” The first to use this phrase was the prophet Hosea who explained that God rejects worship that is separated from ordinary life. Jesus tells us that true religion leads us to serve the poor, the marginalised and to love sinners as our brothers and sisters and as disciples of Christ. This is the “work of mercy” that God prefers to sacrifice offered in a temple. The second reading teaches us that we must imitate Abraham in the constancy of his faith.

SECOND READING    Romans 4:18-25

A reading from the Letter of Saint Paul to the Romans

Brethren: In hope Abraham believed against hope, that he should become the father of many nations; as he had been told, "So shall your descendants be." He did not weaken in faith when he considered his own body, which was as good as dead because he was about a hundred years old, or when he considered the barrenness of Sarah's womb. No distrust made him waver concerning the promise of God, but he grew strong in his faith as he gave glory to God, fully convinced that God was able to do what he had promised. That is why his faith was "reckoned to him as righteousness." But the words, "it was reckoned to him," were written not for his sake alone, but for ours also. It will be reckoned to us who believe in him that raised from the dead Jesus our Lord, who was put to death for our trespasses and raised for our justification.

Year C

Theme of the Sunday: Jesus, the Lord of Life.     The first reading and the gospel tell the story of two women who have lost their only children and are consoled by God who, through an envoy of his, has returned them to life. These two women represent humankind which is powerless against death. Where should people turn to find help? These readings show us that God is the Lord of life. He does not leave people in the grip of death. He raises them up to live for ever. Up to now we may have looked on death with pagan eyes. Let us have courage and change our thinking, as Paul tells us in the second reading.
